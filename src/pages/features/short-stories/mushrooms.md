MUSHROOMS

‘I thought maybe you’d fancy eating out tonight?’

Michelle stood at an angle to Bill’s armchair so as not to block his view of the telly. His ruddy complexion, darkly stubbled because it was the weekend, was looking redder than usual, as if he had woken up angry instead of doing his usual trick of finding something to get steamed up about during the course of the day. She glanced at the screen, trying to read the match score, but without her glasses it was just a blur. The cars through the window were clearer. Bill was proud of their move to the country but really theirs was just a small house stranded on a busy road. ‘At the pub?’ she prompted.

‘I’m not deaf and the answer is no.’

‘I just thought – ‘

‘It’s my bloody birthday and I’d prefer to eat at my own table. If it’s all the same to you.’

Michelle bit her lip. It wasn’t all the same to her, clearly, or she wouldn’t have asked the question. The pub food was dire, but going out would have made a nice change. And besides, they wouldn’t eat at the table, they would have a TV dinner, as they always did. She braced herself, reaching for the cheerfulness that was always there, if she dug deep enough. It *was* his birthday. ‘In that case, any special requests?’

Bill threw her a smile that was like the ring of a distant bell. For a moment she was back at the cousin’s wedding where they had met ten years before, Bill loping towards her across the dance floor, his long arms swinging with intent. Her skin had been in one of its terrible phases and she had fluttered her hands nervously in front of her face as he approached. But Bill had pulled her into the melee without missing a beat, and when the music slowed he kissed her hungrily. His mouth had tasted acrid – whisky, tobacco, a trace of old garlic, but Michelle didn’t mind. She probably tasted bad too. How could anyone know their own taste? To have a partner for once, such an enthusiastic one, had felt like heaven. I am going to fall for him, she had decided, there and then. For what was love ever going to be for someone like her except a decision?

‘I’ve got some lovely salmon fillets. I could poach them in wine and cream.’ Michelle sneaked a glance at her watch. It was time she was off to her mum’s – a round trip of two hours, if the traffic was good.

The TV screen flickered and froze. Michelle held her breath. The satellite dish had been playing up lately. It made Bill mad, especially during a game. She could see his jaw clamping. But then the players started moving again and when he spoke she could hear the happiness in his voice. He was just like a big kid really, in his better moments. ‘I fancy that steak thing with mushrooms and cream, the one you used to do, back in the day when you still loved me properly instead of finding it a chore.’ 

‘You mean stroganoff?’ Michelle managed to keep her voice bright.

‘That’s the one. Lots of mushrooms.’

Michelle retreated to the kitchen. She slid the pack of salmon fillets from the fridge into her handbag to give to her mother and made a mental shopping list for the stroganoff. She had cream and brandy, but would have to get some decent steak and mushrooms. And basmati would go perfectly, Michelle decided, picturing, with a small burst of happiness, exactly how the dish would look: a plump circle of fluffed, steaming rice, the meat and juices a bubbling lake inside, mushrooms popping out all over the place, a mini sprig of parsley to top it all off.

She had her coat on when Bill yelled for tea. Michelle put the kettle on and checked on the birthday cake she had made the day before, moist and chocolatey, just as Bill liked it. She patted the tin lid back with a sigh of satisfaction. She would do the decorating when she got back, something simple with a big loopy *Happy Birthday* in the centre. She loved baking best of all. Once she had even dreamed of running her own tea-shop, a brightly lit, cosy place with pagodas of homemade goodies piled in the windows – flapjacks, cup-cakes, scones, tartlets. But it would have taken money she didn’t have and then life got in the way, what with her Dad’s accident and her mother’s early onset, not to mention Bill getting funny if she showed interest in anything outside of home.

‘Does a man have to die of thirst?’

‘Coming.’ Michelle hurried with the kettle, splashing her fingers. She sucked them as she stirred sugar in with the other hand. She had tried to leave once, a couple of years after the move out of town. Bill had watched in silence from the bedroom doorway as she packed, head cocked, leaning so casually she had really thought for several glorious minutes that he didn’t mind. But then he grabbed her arm as she walked past, twisting it round and up her back till she thought her shoulder would snap. ‘You’re my wife,’ he had hissed. ‘MY wife. And you’re going nowhere.’

There was no bruising, and even in the heat of the moment Michelle had thought, he’s right, I am his wife, what on earth do I think I’m doing? Marriage was about choosing each other, for better, for worse. And where would she go anyway, with her face and no job and her mother in assisted living and on a shoestring budget of her own.

Bill eyed her handbag. ‘Abandoning me again, I see.’

Michelle held it tighter, fearing for the salmon fillets. Bill might mind, he might not. One never knew. ‘Saturday is Mum Day,’ she reminded him cosily. ‘I’ll pick up some nice steak on the way back. Be as quick as I can.’ She shot him a little wave before scooting out of the door. Life was an act, she reflected, slumping behind the wheel of the car. A grand show. Everyone did it. It was nothing odd. Nothing to be ashamed of.

Her mum was cranky and kept her too long. Worse still, she somehow drove back through town forgetting to shop, which meant pinning all her hopes on the small local store a couple of miles from their house. Mercifully, they had basmati and a pack of fillet steak. But no mushrooms. The man stacking shelves laughed when Michelle exclaimed her dismay. ‘Crazy, isn’t it,’ he said merrily, ‘when the woods round here are full of them.’

‘Are they?’ Michelle brought the man’s face into focus. She didn’t recall seeing him before. He had messed up sandy hair and freckles in the wrinkles round his eyes.

‘Yeah, I’ve seen loads this week out with my dog. Mainly those oyster ones. They like to grow on trees.’ He grinned.

Michelle smiled back. ‘So where exactly did you see them?’ She did her best to sound jaunty, as if it was a matter of passing interest rather than something that might rescue her day.

It felt peaceful in the woods, under the rustling canopy of trees. Michelle picked her way along the muddy path, marvelling that she was in a patch of woodland she must have driven past a thousand times. A dog would be nice, she mused, a big, bounding sort, full of all the energy and enthusiasm she was finding it increasingly hard to muster. But Bill wouldn’t like it. He’d say it was a child-substitute. Or something along those lines. Something crushing. No, she could never share the thought with Bill.

Michelle was on the point of despair when she rounded a bend in the path and there they were, clusters of creamy parasols, sticking out of a tree right under her nose. She touched them tenderly, loving how life could flourish in impossible places. In fact oyster mushrooms weren’t ideal for a stroganoff; they fried down to nothing in a trice and had a subtle flavour that might well be drowned by the brandy and cream. But it was getting so late and tugging gently at the fibrous stems, Michelle felt nothing but gratitude. She filled first her coat pockets and then her handbag, partly to be sure of having enough and partly for the sheer joy of picking. To be a forager! It felt so wild and good.

Turning back down the path, she caught sight of a dab of crimson in the undergrowth. Parting the grass with her toe revealed a ring of tiny toadstools, red-capped and white-spotted, as perfect as an illustration in a fairy-tale. Michelle’s heart quickened. That they were poisonous she was in no doubt. Probably even touching them would be dangerous. She crouched down, staring till the red and white caps blurred to a pink, her mind racing. All good things came to those who wait, her father used to say. And look where that had got him.

Back home the dark windows told her Bill had either fallen asleep or was out. Seeing the empty chair and the telly off, Michelle nonetheless experienced a stab of familiar hurt. He would have gone to the pub, spurred on by the fact that it was what she had suggested. He was like that with so many things, uncovering her needs and then deliberately thwarting them. She was his entertainment, Michelle realised bitterly, the way he got his kicks. Not rising to it – not showing she cared – was all she could manage in return.

Switching on the lights, she strode into the kitchen and set to work, pulling on her rubber gloves to wash her pickings from the wood, carefully patting them dry and chopping them into a pile on a board. Remembering Bill was out, she flicked on the radio and hummed to music as she set about slicing up the steak. The contact with food soothed her as it always did; the simple act of producing something nourishing with her own hands. The treat of icing the cake she saved till last. As the joy of the job overtook her she forgot all plans for simplicity and crafted trellises as intricate as spider webs, studding them with pink and yellow flowers, each tiny petal squeezed into being between her fingertips. It looked like a Monet picture when she was done.

Glancing at the kitchen clock, Michelle let out a small cry. Seven o clock. Bill would have had several pints, and that was never good. She turned off the radio and raced round the kitchen tidying up. Bill hated mess. Surfaces cleaned, she took the full rubbish bag out to the wheelie bin. For once the security light came on, otherwise she might not have noticed the ladder, at full stretch against the pebble dash wall housing their bedroom window. Michelle’s first thought was burglars. But the window was closed and above it the satellite dish gleamed like a full moon, alerting her to the fact that the crumpled dark pile at the bottom of the ladder was Bill.

Michelle drove behind the ambulance, watching its blue flashing light in a trance. When the ambulance stopped, she stopped. When it took off, she followed. She would never have found the hospital otherwise. All she could see was Bill’s head, the pulpy forehead and bulging eyes, the matted blood in his hair. She hadn’t tried to move him – not that she could have managed it – but had fetched their bedroom duvet to keep him warm till the ambulance arrived, holding his hand and telling him everything would be all right.

Such accidents were sadly all too common the nurse told her, after Bill had been taken for treatment and Michelle had explained about the faulty dish. She was shown to a chair and given some tea. Seconds later it felt like, the same nurse woke her from a doze. At her elbow was a grave-faced doctor who softly imparted the news that Bill had suffered a massive head trauma and was in a coma from which he was unlikely to emerge. ‘One day at a time,’ he said, ‘but I am afraid the prognosis is not good. You’re best off going home and getting some rest.’

‘Do you have someone you could call?’ the nurse asked.

Michelle blinked at their two faces, recognising for the first time the utter barrenness of her married life; not just childless, but friendless too. Bill had seen to that. He had wanted her all to himself he said. He called it love, but what sort of love meant locking a person away?

Back home the mushrooms were a silky white mountain on the chopping board. Beside them the strips of steak gleamed. The sieve of basmati still sat in the sink, fresh from rinsing. Michelle turned for the stairs and then swung back, beginning slowly, steadily to assemble the meal. She put the rice on to boil, took the cream from the fridge, the brandy from the cupboard, poured oil into a frying pan. As she moved the usual peace started to fill her, like balm. She attended to the mushrooms last, giving them a quick pre-fry before tipping them in with the meat. They were soon submerged, glistening. Michelle stirred the mixture gently, recalling in wonder her moment of near-madness in the woods, the tremble in her knees as she had stepped over the ring of deadly red fungi and walked on. She lowered her face over the pan, drinking in the rich aromas, the brandy a whisper inside. Waiting could indeed bring good things. Eventually.

At the first mouthful, Michelle groaned out loud. The flavours were so much better than she had dared hope, the mushrooms a nutty hint, holding their own within the rich sauce, each morsel of meat as succulent as a chocolate truffle, sliding over her tongue. God. She ate in slow-motion, savouring every texture, every tang.

When her plate was empty she fetched the cake and a big knife. The sugared trellises and flowers shuddered as the blade sliced through them, parting the dark moist sponge to expose the smooth thick ribbon of vanilla butter at the cake’s heart. Michelle cut a fat piece and ate with her fingers, closing her eyes as her teeth sank through the sweet crust into the chocolatey softness underneath. It felt as if she had never fed herself until that moment. Each mouthful was like a bite into freedom. Each swallow an ecstasy.

Into the middle of it all drifted the man with the sandy hair and freckled eyes. He needed thanking, Michelle decided, for the tip about the mushrooms. She would doll up for once, put in her contacts, give him a grin just like the one he’d given her.
