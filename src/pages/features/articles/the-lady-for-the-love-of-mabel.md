ARTICLE FOR ‘THE LADY’ MAGAZINE

June 2019

Looking back, I can see a perfect storm was coming right at me. At the time, newly divorced after a twenty-five year marriage, I was too busily determined to look ahead to notice anything very much. Think positive was my mantra. I kept busy and resolutely cheerful. My career happened to be in the doldrums as well; a novel that had taken three years to write had yet to find a publisher. After fifteen published books this was dispiriting, but I told myself I was lucky not to have hit such a patch before. On the family front, my mother, was growing a little fragile mentally, but remained pretty robust for her eighty one years. She needed more daily help and reassurance, but I was confident my siblings and I could provide it for her. Her own mother had had a similarly bumpy final fifteen years, eventually dying peacefully in her mid-nineties, so there was no need to panic.

Most reassuring and exciting of all, a new man had come into my life. He was married, but we fell instantly and deeply in love, and he quickly promised to leave his wife so that we could spend the rest of our lives together – not an ideal scenario, but life has to be messy sometimes, especially where matters of the heart are concerned, and even more especially once you hit your fifties. By that stage in the dating game everybody has baggage. Just as everybody is starting to recognise that time is limited and chances at happiness are rare and to be grabbed.

As a novelist, I prided myself on understanding such scenarios. After all, I had spent three decades writing about them - the twists and torments of human emotions, characters going through hoops but finding their just desserts, reaping what they sow. I knew myself to be an essentially good and decent person. I was certain that my own happy ending awaited me. It was just a question of hanging on in there. Keeping that glass half full. It is only looking back – oh, the miracle of hindsight - that I can see I was like someone standing on a beach while a tsunami rolled in from the sea behind her. I wasn’t looking in the right direction. I wasn’t looking anywhere. I was a sitting duck.

I was on my way to a leg wax when my mother collapsed. The doctors gave her twenty four hours, but she took her last breath while I was still stuck in nose-to-tail traffic on the A23. My sister phoned me in the car with the news, though I knew before I answered what she would say. It didn’t stop me breaking motorway speed limits to get the hospital bedside, half of me imagining that I might arrive in time for some transitional only-just-dead phase, that a farewell of sorts would be possible. I got there an hour later. It was my first face-to-face encounter with a death and the word ‘departed’ could not be more apt. Mum was utterly gone. The stiff indifference of her body in my arms haunts me still.

Through my grief I tried to focus on the positive. There was much to celebrate. Mum had been a devoted wife (my father had died twenty years before) and the most loving of parents. Her life had been rich and extraordinary. I even found myself volunteering to do the funeral tribute in order to do full justice to it. It remains one of the hardest tasks I have ever set myself, but I held it together – just – and it went well. Afterwards I was almost euphoric. I felt invincible.

Then, a few months later, the married man said we were finished, that he would be staying with his wife after all, and the tsunami hit. I broke down. Losing my marriage and my mother, it had felt as if a chunk of my past was gone; now, with this new loss it was as if my future had been blown away too. Grief seems too small a word for this. I lost all sense of who I was, where I was going or why. I was a dot in an ocean. I didn’t matter to anyone, least of all myself. Getting out of bed seemed pointless, as did eating, or taking care of myself. All I was good for was weeping, copiously, frequently, embarrassingly. Sometimes it felt as if every sadness I had ever experienced had coalesced into one big whirlpool, sucking me down.

One of the worst things about acute unhappiness is that it robs you of self-confidence. You feel unloved, ugly, and unlovable. You go nowhere, but you are exhausted. It also shatters your ability to concentrate. So there was no question of writing for solace, or reading for that matter, or even watching telly. For once, the only story I was immersed in – in the last way I wanted – was my own.

Close family and friends were saintly in their efforts to rally round. During the early, dire weeks they took it in turns to visit, ring and generally cajole me back into the real world. They reminded me of what each of us needs to know – that we are loved, that we do matter. They all deserve medals for how they helped. However, I knew this priceless support could not continue forever. They each had busy lives to return to. Somehow I had to find a new purpose for myself, a new path, a new diversion from my woes.

Cue Mabel, a Golden Doodle of such eye-popping beauty that, aged two, she still turns heads wherever we go. The idea of getting a dog flew into my head one day from nowhere, a mad, impossible idea that gave me an excuse to look at cute images of puppies – an exercise which in itself, as scientists have proven, is guaranteed to boost serotonin levels. But somehow, one thing led to another and a few weeks later I found myself paying a large deposit on an as yet unborn litter of Golden Doodles. Crucially, it was a refundable deposit, so I could still kid myself that I wouldn’t go through with it. Friends were equally relieved to hear this. A puppy seemed a dodgy attempt at finding happiness. I was still fragile. I needed to get out more, not tie myself to a dog. But deep inside me some sort of blind instinct was kicking in, some inner sense of how to distract my tattered heart in exactly the way it needed.

Then came the day to meet the puppies. I defy anyone not to enjoy being crawled on by six-week old Golden Doodles, each a bundle of fluff; but from the start it was Mabel who caught my eye. She was literally the cutest living creature I had ever laid eyes on and, to my great joy, seemed pretty keen on me too. While her siblings tried to chew my flip-flops, she toddled up to lick my hands and arms and then promptly rolled on her back, inviting me to tickle her hairless pink pot of a tum. Her fur was the colour of clotted cream with apricot tips to her ears and tail, and her eyes were treacle brown, earnest and trusting. The decision to take her home was instantaneous and exhilarating.

Loving Mabel remains easypeasy. She has the sweetest nature – just enough wickedness, with lashings of lovingkindness. Which isn’t to say that it has all been plain-sailing. Training a puppy – so essential in the mayhem of London – takes stamina. There have been ups, and some downs. The commitment is as all-consuming as I expected – think childcare with a child who can never fully be trusted – a squirrel, or reachable food and Mabel doesn’t give me a backward glance. A safe, happy dog takes energy and vigilance. What you get in return is love. Unconditional, unquestioning, every moment of every hour of every day.

It sounds cheesy to say Mabel has ‘saved’ me. But she has. I was an optimist who had lost her optimism, a romantic who had lost her romance, a daughter who had lost her mum, a writer who had lost her inspiration. And then Mabel came along. She keeps me fit, makes me laugh, takes me to new places and finds me friends. Just looking at her makes my heart sing. She likes everybody but me best of all. Since getting her my life has taken off again on all fronts. She has even inspired a book. It is almost as if Mabel’s boundless energy levels have infused mine. I have no idea what the future holds or whether I will even get another dog. All I know is that I am on a new path in my journey and that it is Mabel who is by my side.
